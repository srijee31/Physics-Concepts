# Newton's Laws of Motion

## Introduction

The laws of motion were formulated by the scientist **Isaac Newton**.  
These laws describe the relationship between the motion of an object and the forces acting on it.

Newton proposed **three fundamental laws of motion** that form the foundation of classical mechanics.

---

## Force

A **force** is a push or pull that can change the state of motion of an object.

Examples:

- Pushing a door
- Pulling a rope
- Gravity pulling objects toward Earth

The SI unit of force is **Newton (N)**.

---

## Newton's First Law of Motion

Newton’s First Law is also known as the **Law of Inertia**.

Statement:

An object will remain **at rest or continue moving with constant velocity** unless acted upon by an external force.

Example:

- A book on a table will remain at rest until someone moves it.
- A moving bicycle will keep moving until friction or brakes stop it.

### Inertia

Inertia is the **tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion**.

Types of inertia:

1. Inertia of rest  
2. Inertia of motion  
3. Inertia of direction  

---

## Newton's Second Law of Motion

Newton’s Second Law explains how force affects motion.

Statement:

The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the applied force.

This leads to the famous formula:

Force = Mass × Acceleration

F = m × a

Where:

- F → Force
- m → Mass
- a → Acceleration

Example:

If a force of 10 N acts on a mass of 2 kg:

Acceleration = F / m  
Acceleration = 10 / 2 = 5 m/s²

---

## Newton's Third Law of Motion

Statement:

For every **action**, there is an **equal and opposite reaction**.

This means forces always occur in pairs.

Example:

- When you push a wall, the wall pushes back on you.
- When a rocket launches, gases push downward while the rocket moves upward.

---

## Key Points

- Newton proposed three laws to explain motion.
- The first law explains inertia.
- The second law explains the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration.
- The third law explains action and reaction forces.
- These laws form the basis of classical mechanics.
