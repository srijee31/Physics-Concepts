# Circular Motion

Circular motion occurs when an object moves along a curved path or a complete circle. Even if the speed is constant, the direction is always changing!

## 1. The Concept of "Changing Direction"
In physics, "velocity" includes both speed and direction. Since an object in a circle is constantly changing its direction, it is technically **accelerating**—even if its speedometer stays at 60 km/h.

* **Centripetal Force ($F_c$):** This is the "center-seeking" force that pulls an object toward the middle of the circle to keep it from flying off in a straight line.
* **The Formula:**
  $$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
  *(Where $m$ is mass, $v$ is velocity, and $r$ is the radius of the circle).*



---

## 2. Real-Life Examples
1. **Turning a Bike:** When you tilt your bike to take a corner in Bengaluru traffic, the friction between your tires and the road provides the centripetal force needed to turn.
2. **The Moon:** Gravity acts as a centripetal force that keeps the Moon orbiting the Earth instead of drifting away into space.
3. **Washing Machines:** During the "spin cycle," the tub rotates fast. The walls of the tub push the clothes inward (centripetal force), but the water flies out through the small holes because it wants to keep moving in a straight line.

---

## 3. Centripetal vs. Centrifugal (The "Fake" Force)
* **Centripetal:** The real force pulling you **inward** (like the string pulling a spinning stone).
* **Centrifugal:** The "feeling" of being pushed **outward** (like being pressed against the car door when taking a fast turn). It’s not a real force; it’s just your body trying to keep moving straight while the car turns.



---

## 4. Key Terms to Remember

| Term | Simple Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Radius ($r$)** | The distance from the center to the edge of the circle. |
| **Period ($T$)** | The time it takes to complete one full circle. |
| **Angular Velocity ($\omega$)** | How fast the object is rotating (measured in radians per second). |
