# 📏 Physical Quantities and Units

## 1. Introduction

Physics is the study of the natural world using **measurements and mathematical descriptions**.  
To describe physical phenomena accurately, scientists use **physical quantities and units**.

A **physical quantity** is any measurable property of a physical system.

Examples include:

- length
- mass
- time
- temperature
- speed
- force

Each physical quantity must be expressed using a **numerical value and a unit**.

Example:

Length = 5 meters


Here:
- 5 → numerical value
- meter → unit

---

## 2. What is a Physical Quantity?

A **physical quantity** is a property that can be measured and expressed with a number and a unit.

Examples:

| Physical Quantity | Example |
|------------------|--------|
| Length | 10 meters |
| Mass | 2 kilograms |
| Time | 5 seconds |
| Temperature | 25°C |

Without units, numbers have **no physical meaning**.

Example:


10 (unclear)
10 meters (clear)


---

## 3. Fundamental and Derived Quantities

Physical quantities are classified into two types:

### 3.1 Fundamental Quantities

Fundamental quantities are **basic quantities that cannot be derived from other quantities**.

There are **seven fundamental quantities** in the SI system.

| Quantity | SI Unit | Symbol |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Length | meter | m |
| Mass | kilogram | kg |
| Time | second | s |
| Electric Current | ampere | A |
| Temperature | kelvin | K |
| Amount of Substance | mole | mol |
| Luminous Intensity | candela | cd |

These are the **building blocks of all physical measurements**.

---

### 3.2 Derived Quantities

Derived quantities are obtained by combining fundamental quantities.

Examples include:

| Derived Quantity | Formula | Unit |
|-----------------|--------|------|
| Velocity | distance / time | m/s |
| Acceleration | velocity / time | m/s² |
| Force | mass × acceleration | Newton (N) |
| Energy | force × distance | Joule (J) |

Example formula for velocity:


Velocity = Distance / Time

v = d / t


---

## 4. SI Units (International System of Units)

The **SI System (Système International d'Unités)** is the standard system used worldwide for scientific measurements.

It ensures that measurements are **consistent and universally understood**.

Example:

- 1 meter in India = 1 meter in the USA
- 1 kilogram in Japan = 1 kilogram in Germany

---

## 5. Scalar and Vector Quantities

Physical quantities can also be classified as **scalars** and **vectors**.

### Scalar Quantities

Scalar quantities have **only magnitude**.

Examples:

- mass
- time
- temperature
- speed
- energy

Example:


Temperature = 30°C


---

### Vector Quantities

Vector quantities have **both magnitude and direction**.

Examples:

- velocity
- force
- acceleration
- displacement

Example:


Velocity = 20 m/s east


Direction is important in vector quantities.

---

## 6. Importance of Units in Physics

Units are essential because they:

- provide a standard way to measure quantities
- allow scientists to compare results
- help maintain consistency in experiments
- make communication of scientific data easier

Without units, scientific measurements would be **meaningless or confusing**.

---

## 7. Real-Life Example

When you drive a car, the speedometer shows your speed.

Example:


Speed = 60 km/h


This means the car travels **60 kilometers in one hour**.

Speed is a **derived quantity** because it is calculated using:


speed = distance / time


---

## 8. Simple Summary

A **physical quantity** is a measurable property expressed with a number and a unit.  
Units provide a standard way to measure and compare physical properties.  
All physical quantities are classified as **fundamental or derived**, and they form the foundation of physics.
