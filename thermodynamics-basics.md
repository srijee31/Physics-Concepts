# 🌡️ Thermodynamics Basics

## 1. Introduction to Thermodynamics

**Thermodynamics** is the branch of physics that deals with the study of heat, energy, and the work done by systems.

It is concerned with how **energy** flows between a system and its surroundings, how energy can change forms (like from heat to work), and how the **temperature** of objects affects their behavior.

---

## 2. The Four Laws of Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is governed by four fundamental laws:

### 2.1 **Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics**

**Example**: When you heat a gas, it expands and does work. The heat energy increases the internal energy of the gas and also does work on the surroundings.

### 2.3 **Second Law of Thermodynamics (Entropy)**

The total entropy (disorder) of an isolated system always increases over time.

**Entropy** measures the amount of **disorder or randomness** in a system.

Example: When you mix hot and cold water, the final temperature becomes **uniform**, increasing the system's entropy.

### 2.4 **Third Law of Thermodynamics**

As the temperature of a system approaches absolute zero (0 K), the entropy approaches a minimum.

This law explains why it is impossible to reach absolute zero in a finite number of steps.

## 3. Work and Heat in Thermodynamics

### 3.1 **Heat (Q)**

Heat is the energy transferred between two systems due to a temperature difference.

- Heat flows from **high temperature to low temperature**.
- Heat can be transferred in three ways: **conduction**, **convection**, and **radiation**.

### 3.2 **Work (W)**

Work is the energy transferred when a system changes due to an external force.

In thermodynamics, work can be related to pressure and volume:
W = PΔV

## 4. Heat Engines and Efficiency

A **heat engine** is a device that converts heat energy into work.

For example, a **steam engine** heats water to produce steam, which then does work by moving a piston.

The **efficiency** of a heat engine is given by:
Efficiency = \frac{W}{Q_{in}} \times 100

## 5. Real-Life Example: Refrigerator

A refrigerator works as a **heat pump**:

1. It **absorbs heat** from inside the fridge (low temperature).
2. It **transfers that heat** to the surroundings (high temperature).

This process **violates the natural direction of heat flow** (second law of thermodynamics) by using **work** to move heat from cold to hot.

---

## 6. Entropy and the Arrow of Time

Entropy is a measure of the **randomness or disorder** in a system. Over time, entropy tends to increase in a closed system.

For example:

- Ice melting into water increases the disorder (entropy).
- A **clean room** will naturally become messy over time.

---
