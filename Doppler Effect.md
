# Doppler Effect

## Introduction
The Doppler Effect refers to the change in frequency (or wavelength) of a wave in relation to an observer who is moving relative to the source of the wave.

This effect is commonly heard in sound waves, but can also be seen in any type of wave (including light).

---

## How It Works

### 1. **Approaching Source:**
- When the source is moving towards the observer, the frequency appears higher, and the pitch seems higher.
  
### 2. **Receding Source:**
- When the source moves away from the observer, the frequency appears lower, and the pitch seems lower.

### Example
- **Siren of an ambulance**: As the ambulance moves closer, the siren gets higher-pitched. As it moves away, the pitch lowers.

---

## Formula for Doppler Effect (Sound Waves)

For sound waves, the Doppler Effect is expressed as:

f' = f ( (v + vo) / (v - vs) )

Where:
- f' = observed frequency
- f = emitted frequency
- v = speed of sound in the medium
- vo = speed of the observer (positive if moving toward the source)
- vs = speed of the source (positive if moving away from the observer)

---

## Applications of Doppler Effect

1. **Radar & SONAR**  
   Used to measure the speed of moving objects (e.g., speed of cars, submarines)

2. **Medical Imaging (Ultrasound)**  
   Measures the flow of blood in arteries

3. **Astronomy**  
   Used to determine the motion of stars and galaxies (redshift and blueshift)

---

## Doppler Shift in Light (Redshift and Blueshift)

The Doppler effect can also be applied to light waves, where:
- **Redshift**: Light moving away from the observer (longer wavelength, towards the red end of the spectrum)
- **Blueshift**: Light moving towards the observer (shorter wavelength, towards the blue end of the spectrum)

---

## Summary

The Doppler Effect explains how the frequency of waves changes based on the relative motion of the source and observer. This phenomenon is crucial in various fields, such as radar, medical imaging, and astronomy.
